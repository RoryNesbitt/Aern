# Aldric Vael

Aldric Vael is the foremost technomancer of the Azzurium Age. His research into the nature of arcane power shapes the empire's infrastructure more profoundly than any other single mind, and his published works represent the most sophisticated writing on the subject ever produced.

## Study

Aldric comes up through the towers of [Ashspire](/expansion/locations/thren/ashspire) at a time when technomancy is still finding the edges of what it can do. He is not the most celebrated student of his intake, by most accounts, but he is the most persistent. Where others are satisfied to learn the applications of arcane power, Aldric wants to understand what it actually is.

His early published work addresses the mechanics of arcane gem charging, the principles of sustainable energy capture, and the theoretical basis for long-distance power transfer. The lightning trains owe their design in no small part to his foundational papers. Artificers across the empire build careers on methods he establishes before his thirtieth year.

He accepts the acclaim with what colleagues describe as genuine puzzlement. The applications are not the interesting part.

## The Divine Question

The conclusion Aldric eventually publishes — that the energy powering the empire's machines is divine in origin, literally the power of the gods spilling from their plane into this one — is not immediately accepted. Academics don't usually agree with priests so it takes some convincing to tell them that the clerics got it before they did.

Once they do, the consensus shifts quickly. The evidence is too coherent to dismiss.

For Aldric, the finding is not troubling. It is clarifying. He has spent years studying an effect. Now he understands the cause. The next question is obvious: if the power comes from the gods, what would a closer study of its source reveal?

## The Towers

Aldric proposes the construction of two dedicated research towers. One on the outskirts of the empire, providing a controlled environment for continued study. One on Ferrath — chosen deliberately for the continent's documented closeness to other planes of existence, including, Aldric believes, the home of the gods themselves.

The proposal is approved. The towers are built. Aldric divides his time between them, producing work that colleagues describe as the furthest reach technomantic scholarship has ever achieved — a genuine attempt to map the relationship between the divine plane and the mortal world, conducted with rigour and evident care.

His notes from this period show a man who is excited. Who feels he is close to something. Who is certain, in the way that careful and brilliant people sometimes become certain, that he understands what he is doing.
