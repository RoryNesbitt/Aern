# Ashspire

Ashspire is the temple of # Ashspire

Ashspire is the temple of [Dati](/deities/estasi#dati) — the Scholar, keeper of all knowledge and history — and the centre of learning in the empire. It is a self-contained town that exists entirely in service of that temple, governing itself, funding itself, and answering to no external authority.

To study at Ashspire is to study under [Dati](/deities/estasi#dati). The two are not distinguished. Research is worship here, and the scholars who fill its halls are as much priests as they are academics. The towers they built, the knowledge they accumulate, the students they teach — all of it is understood as devotion to the same god, expressed through the work itself.

## The Temple

The temple at Ashspire's centre is not a building that also houses a university. It is a temple whose practice happens to look, from the outside, like academic study. The distinction matters to those inside it.

[Dati](/deities/estasi#dati)'s domain is knowledge — all of it, in every form. His priests pursue it accordingly. The temple's walls are filled with centuries of accumulated records, research, and teaching. New knowledge is added constantly. Old knowledge is preserved, studied, and built upon. The work is never finished because the domain is infinite, which suits everyone here rather well.

Students come to Ashspire to learn, and in doing so become part of that practice. They are students of the temple as much as students of any subject. Those who stay long enough, and are found worthy, may become priests in their own right — researchers and teachers who have made the pursuit of knowledge the centre of their lives rather than a stage in them.

## Governance

Ashspire is governed by its senior priest, who holds authority over the temple and everything within it. A council of senior priests — each distinguished in their own field of study — advises on significant decisions. Tower assignments, admissions, the direction of major research projects: these are considered carefully and collectively, with the senior priest's word as final.

The temple sustains itself through student fees and donations from former students, patrons, and those across the empire with an interest in what Ashspire produces. It receives no imperial funding and is not required to account for itself to Trademoor or anyone else. This independence is not incidental — it is understood within the temple as a condition of doing the work properly.

## Admission

The temple controls who studies at Ashspire. Admission is open to anyone found suitable by the priests — no imperial filtering, no formal preference for background or wealth. Those who are accepted come from across the empire and occasionally beyond it.

What the temple is looking for is genuine. A hunger for knowledge that will not be satisfied by a comfortable career is more valuable here than exceptional preparation or the right connections. Those who arrive with the former and lack the latter tend to do well. Those who arrive with only the latter tend to find Ashspire disappointing and leave.

## The Towers

Seven towers rise across Ashspire's grounds, visible from the train long before the rest of the town comes into view. They were built by the temple's priests as spaces for closer communion with [Dati](/deities/estasi#dati) — the belief being that height brought a researcher nearer to the divine, and that the work conducted there would be guided accordingly.

Most students never enter one. Tower assignment is the senior priest's judgement that a person's work and character have reached a point where the deeper practice the towers represent is appropriate. Some spend entire careers at Ashspire without receiving it. Those who do describe the experience in terms that are difficult to convey to those who haven't.

## The Town

Beyond the temple, Ashspire is spare. Accommodation, a market kept stocked by regular shipments from Trademoor, places to eat and gather. The infrastructure of a community that exists for one reason and has organised itself accordingly. Those who come expecting a city find it insufficient. Those who come for the work find it exactly enough.
